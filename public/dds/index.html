Designing And Documenting A Design System To Enable The Team To Focus On User Experience Rather Than Pixels.

  • Audit
  • Design Principles
  • Visual Direction
  • Structure
  • Components
  • Documentation
  • Conclusion
Digima, a web-based CRM application, provides small to medium size companies with a way to understand their customers' needs. A few months after I joined the company, we came to a realization: Digima was getting harder and harder to scale. It got so bad that the decision was made to rebuild the product from the ground up.

01 | Problems

  • The company needed a more scalable way to deliver feature designs.
  • Digima users needed a more consistent user experience.

02 | Goals

  • Create tools and processes that can deliver quality, consistency, and speed to the design process.
  • Redesign the product using these tools.

03 | Role and responsibilities

  • UI design
  • Project management
  • Build and maintain design system

04 | Outcomes

  • An entire new design system that included a dedicated documentation site was created.
  • Feature design quality, consistency, and delivery time were significantly imporved.

Audit

The product audit revealed many inconsistencies and user experiences issues, confirming the need for a design system.

To understand the needs of the redesign, I first had to evaluate the current product.

To that end, I went through the entire product interface and took screenshots of menus, buttons, and modals. I created a Google spreadsheet where I grouped each component into categories and gave each one a name.

Viewing all the components side by side confirmed why we needed a redesign. There were 130 different colors, 56 different shades of greys, 22 different font sizes and 4 completely different variants of search fields.

It became clear that what we needed was a well organized, collaborative design system custom-built to meet the companys needs.

Design Principles

Agreeing on what kind of product experience we wanted to deliver to our users helped us stay on track when making difficult decisions.

Before I could start exploring visual directions for the new design, the team and I needed to decide on our core principles.

These core principles are meant to act as our north star when making difficult product decisions and are the basis around which the product should be built.

After a few discussions, we came up with the following four principles.

  • Be purposeful: Always design with intention and seek to validate your solutions with data when possible.
  • Provide clarity: Ensuring consistent, cohesive user experiences by applying the same solutions to the same problems. Changing patterns and workflows familiar to existing users only when doing so is a definite improvement. (consistency trump originality and coherence trump consistency).
  • Design Iteratively: No one solution will be perfect the first time. Be patient, learn from feedback, and improve user experience Iteratively.
  • Foster collaboration: Software development is a team sport. A design cannot exist without the support of others, so learning to implicate them into your process will only make for better designs.

Visual Direction

Before I could start redesigning the product and building the design system, we needed to decide on a visual direction.

Once we had an idea of what kind of product experience we wanted to deliver, I started iterating on a few potential design directions for the main screens of the application.

After a few rounds of feedbacks, the team and I ultimately selected one final style.

Structure

An agreed-upon and straightforward structure for our design system helped promote the shared understanding necessary to reach our goal of improving efficiency.

Now that I had an idea of what the overall solution will look like, it was time to get into the details of how the design system will be structured.

I started by organizing and categorizing every component identified during the current product versions audit. During my conversion with developers, I noticed that often, they were using different names when referring to UI elements.

Since one of the goals of the design system was to create a shared understanding, it was essential to decide on the right names collectively and grouped families of UI components based on their similarities.

Components

Designing the components was a long and meticulous process, but one that will significantly increase feature design quality and delivery time.

Once the design system structure was defined, I got to work converting the UI elements listed during the audit phase to the new design style established earlier.

To keep changes isolated and performance high, each UI element was created as a single Sketch library. This organization, however, made it difficult to quickly navigate through the entire set, so to address this issue, I also created a master file that acted as an entry point.

One of the ways I could fulfill my goal of improving efficiency was to reduce guesswork as much as possible when adding or modifying a component. To that end, I created a set of templates, rules, and conventions, describing how a component library should be built and maintained.

Documentation

The documentation site helped the team move from design to implementation quickly by eliminating guess-work.

To forge a shared understanding of the system between designer and developer and facilitate implementation, we needed a documentation site. With the help of the development team, I started working on creating the website and adding components to it.

To help designers quickly move between the design files and the documentation, I decided to mirror the navigation, structure, and terminology in the Sketch files and the documentation site. I also added a link to the component documentation in the sketch files themselves and a link to the sketch library in the related documentation article.

Developers could quickly inspect any component by simply clicking on its preview right from the documentation site itself.

When available, I also made sure to display the react version of a component as well as provide code snippets for it.

Conclusion

Creating a design system was necessary to address the scaling and consistency issues that led to a product redesign.

This project turned out to be a massive undertaking spanning more than a year and involving several departments.

However, these efforts paid off:

  • As a designer, it allowed me to create mockups in a fraction of the time and with a higher level of precision than ever before; it also taught me the depth and complexity of thinking involved in crafting every little piece of an applications user experience.
  • For developers, being able to rely on clear documentation and reuse existing patterns allows them to focus on writing tests and efficient code rather than continually tweaking CSS values.
  • For the business, the productivity gains weve seen in new feature development have more than justified the investments we made in adopting the system.
